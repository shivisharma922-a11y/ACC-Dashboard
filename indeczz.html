05 · KEY STRESS TEST INSIGHTS
🛡
Defensive Beta Cushions Downside
A portfolio beta of 0.625 — held constant across all scenarios — acts as a structural shock absorber. When the market falls 20 pp from SC0 to SC5, the portfolio loses only 19 pp, confirming the below-market sensitivity inherent to FMCG stocks. The low beta is the primary reason the portfolio outperforms its benchmark in crisis conditions on a relative basis, even as it turns negative in absolute terms.
⚠
Alpha Turns Negative After Medium Stress
Under normal conditions, the portfolio generates +3.04% excess alpha over the CAPM expected return — a signal of active management skill. However, even moderate regulatory tightening (SC4) erodes this entirely, pushing alpha to −1.96%. This inflection point at SC4 indicates that the portfolio's outperformance is fragile under structural shocks and not merely market-driven. Stock selection advantage disappears when macro conditions deteriorate.
📉
Sharpe Ratio Becomes Negative in Crisis
The Sharpe ratio collapses from +0.642 → −0.140 → −0.470 across scenarios. A negative Sharpe implies the portfolio earns below the risk-free rate on a volatility-adjusted basis — effectively destroying capital in real terms. This is particularly severe in SC5 where −0.470 signals that excess return is negative and risk is simultaneously elevated. Investors holding to preserve capital would need to exit or hedge at SC4 onset.
🔥
Volatility Clustering Worsens Risk-Adjusted Returns
Volatility expands from 13.5% → 18.5% → 22.0% — a +63% increase from normal to crisis. This is consistent with volatility clustering, where stressed markets exhibit prolonged elevated variance. Rising volatility simultaneously compresses both the Sharpe and Treynor ratios by increasing the denominator, creating a double penalty: falling returns and rising risk amplify risk-adjusted underperformance non-linearly.
⚖
Treynor Ratio Collapse Confirms Systematic Risk Exposure
The Treynor ratio drops from +13.87 → −4.14 → −16.54, a fall of 30.41 points. Since Treynor uses beta (not total volatility) as its denominator, this confirms that the collapse is driven by systematic risk materialising, not idiosyncratic noise. The magnitude of −16.54 in SC5 is disproportionate and signals that the FMCG portfolio's market-linked return suffers catastrophically once systemic stress exceeds moderate levels.
🎯
Portfolio Outperforms Market Benchmark in All Scenarios
Across all three scenarios, portfolio return exceeds market return: +15.67% vs 16.0% at SC0 (near-equal), +4.41% vs 6.0% at SC4 (portfolio outperforms on downside), and −3.34% vs −4.0% at SC5 (portfolio loses less). The consistent margin above market during stress validates the defensive FMCG tilt and suggests the portfolio is structurally positioned for capital preservation rather than aggressive upside capture.
